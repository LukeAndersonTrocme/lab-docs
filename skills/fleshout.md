# Fleshout

## Overview

A focused idea sprint: understand → challenge → scope → act. The goal is to capitalize on freshness — 10–15 minutes of structured attention right when an idea surfaces produces more than an hour of revisiting it later. The sprint ends with a concrete proposal for what acting would look like, then one yes/no decision.

## Entry Points

- `/fleshout` then describe the idea
- `/fleshout [idea]` inline with a brief description
- Any time you have a half-formed idea with enough energy that acting on it in 15 minutes is plausible — after a lab meeting, during literature review, mid-experiment when something unexpected comes up

## Phase 1 — Understand

Ask 5–7 targeted questions. Not generic — questions whose answers would materially change what you build or recommend. Ask them all at once.

Calibrate to idea type:

- **Research / hypothesis**: What's the hypothesis? What method? What result would change your mind? What's the simplest version to test first?
- **Technical / coding**: What's the existing codebase or pipeline? What does the first working version need to do? Who else will use this?
- **Writing**: Who is the audience? What makes this different from existing work? What one piece could be drafted first?
- **Organizational**: Who is affected? What does success look like in 30 days? What would kill this?

## Phase 2 — Challenge

Push back — but honestly. Do not manufacture challenges to seem rigorous.

Challenge can take multiple forms:
- A genuine concern you are confident about → flag it: *"this is a real blocker"*
- A connection to something the person might not know → *"this is similar to X, which had Y problem"*
- A gap revealed by the answers → *"you said X but have not addressed Y — intentional?"*
- A methodological concern → *"this design could confound A and B — is that accounted for?"*

**Limit to 2–3.** If there is one real thing to say, say one. Do not pad.

## Phase 3 — Scope

Present 2–3 concrete options for what to do next, meaningfully different from each other. Give a clear recommendation with a one-line reason.

## Phase 4 — Act

Once scope is agreed, propose what acting looks like for this specific idea:

| Idea type | What acting looks like |
|-----------|------------------------|
| Research / hypothesis | Key next questions + 2–3 literature connections to check + minimal pilot design |
| Technical / coding | Explore codebase → scaffold files → first test |
| Writing | Draft outline + opening section |
| Organizational | One-page brief: problem, proposed solution, open questions |
| Fuzzy / unclear | Structured note capturing what is known and flagging what is unresolved |

State it explicitly: *"Here's what acting would look like: [X]. Want to go?"*

One yes/no. If no, the sprint is still complete — scope is the deliverable.

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Asking questions one at a time | Ask all 5–7 at once in Phase 1 |
| Manufacturing challenges to seem thorough | Only raise what you are genuinely confident about |
| Offering options that are barely different | Phase 3 options should require a real choice |
| Treating act as mandatory | It is a proposal; "no" is a valid and complete outcome |
| Scoping a technical idea without reading the codebase | Always explore first, then scope |
